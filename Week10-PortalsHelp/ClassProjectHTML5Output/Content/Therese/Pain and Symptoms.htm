Pain and Symptoms

Describing Pain

Pain Scale

The use of a pain scale is meant to make the description of pain “objective”.  It is difficult for someone contending with chronic pain to be accurate because you may have developed coping mechanisms or a tolerance for pain.  Pain, which is very subjective, is also affected by fatigue, stress, trigger points, sensitivity to stimuli, etc.

The pain scale below is an example.  In addition to giving the number, define to the doctor what that number means to you.  For example, what is a 6 for you, the doctor may think is an 8 with additional description.  Pain is very relative.  Know that individuals may report different severities of pain for the same types of injuries, making the doctor’s job challenging.

Mankoski Pain Scale

This scale was developed by Andrea Mankoski in 19951 Pain scale adapted from the scale posted on http://www.ChronicallyAwesome.org.  It is a scale of 1 to 10, with 10 being the highest level of pain.  This scale is meant to describe the ability to function in your whole life.

Pain is making you cry or moan.  You may be unable to speak.  Strongest pain killers are only partially effective.  In bed all day, unable to rest comfortably or function

10

Pain is unmanageable.  Strongest painkillers are ineffective.  You may pass out from the pain or feel the need to go to the Emergency Room.

 

Provide Adequate Details

Successful control and/or diagnosis of the pain is often more likely to be achieved if a good description of the pain is made. This description should include:

  • The site of the pain

  • The duration and speed of onset

  • Whether the pain is intermittent (waves, flashes) or constant

  • The character of the pain:

  • Aching

  • Prickling

  • Splitting

  • Sore

  • Tender

  • Pounding

  • Crampy

  • Dull

  • Itchy

  • Tingling

  • sharp

  • Burning

  • Stabbing

  • Aching

  • Throbbing, beating

  • Hot

  • Tearing

  • Crushing

  • Localized (stays in one place)

  • Radiating (pain spreads to other parts of the body)

  • What aggravates the pain? (movement, pressure, light, noise, weight-bearing)

  • What relieves the pain? (ice, heat, compression, elevation, immobilization, medication)

  • Does it impact your daily living?  Do you do things differently or avoid doing certain things because of the pain?

  • Does the pain affect you socially or emotionally?

  • Severity - use of pain scale.

Pain is very relative. Individuals may report different severities of pain for the same types of injuries. Some patients after surgery request as much pain medication as they can legally have, while others only require a Tylenol.

Describing symptoms

Give Health Practitioners enough detail to understand your symptoms. When you say something like, “I’m tired” the health practitioner is imagining how they feel when they are tired. What do you mean by tired? 

Example of a Good Description:

“I am so tired that I have difficulty getting out of bed, I usually require 2 one-hour naps each day, and I go to bed at 9:30 pm. I average 9 hours of sleep a night but never feel refreshed. This seems to have started about two months ago after a camping trip. I have tried adjusting my bedtime routines but it hasn’t helped. I am most tired in the morning and drink three cups of coffee in order to feel like I’m awake. By mid-morning, I’m so tired I need to lay down to take a nap. This happens again around 3 or 4 in the afternoon”

Things to Note

You may want to record the following information about your symptoms to report to your health practitioner.  Following this are 10 Symptom Record Forms.

  • How long ago did the symptom(s) start?

  • Was there a precipitating event?  What were the circumstances?

  • Is there a time of day the symptom starts?

  • Is there a time of day the symptom bothers you most?

  • Does the symptom come and go during the day?

  • Is the symptom influenced by any of the following? 

    • Activity

    • Rest

    • Stress

    • Eating pattern or particular food

    • Prescription or non-prescription medication (which one(s)?)

    • Alcohol, Caffeine, or Smoking

    • Environmental exposure (pet hair, cigarette smoke, strong scent, cleaning product, etc.)

  • What have you tried to help with the symptom?  Has anything been more or less effective than other things?

  • On a scale of 1 to 10, how much does the symptom interfere with your daily living activities? (See p. Mankoski Pain Scale)
